# Problem Statement

Young amputees in sub-Saharan Africa — particularly those aged 15–30 who lost limbs due to agricultural accidents, landmines, or illness — have virtually no pathway into adaptive sport. Conventional prosthetics available in the region are functional but rigid, designed for walking and basic mobility, not dynamic physical activity. Magnetic osseointegration prosthetics (where the limb attaches directly to a titanium implant in the bone via magnetic coupling) offer transformational capability — responsiveness, stability, and the range of motion needed for sport — but cost between $15,000–$70,000 and are only fitted at specialist clinics in Europe and North America.

The result: a generation of young amputees in countries like Rwanda, Uganda, and Kenya who are physically capable of competing in adaptive sports but are locked out entirely — not by their bodies, but by cost, geography, and the absence of any local fitting or support infrastructure. Their aspiration has no product built for them.

**Target segment:** Young amputees in sub-Saharan Africa, aged 15–30, with sporting ambition and no access to advanced prosthetic technology.
