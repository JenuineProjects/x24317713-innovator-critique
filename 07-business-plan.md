# Business Plan & Marketing Campaign

## MagnaStep — Kigali, Rwanda

---

## Executive Summary

MagnaStep is a B-Corp certified startup headquartered in Kigali, Rwanda, building high-performance magnetic sport prosthetics for young amputees across sub-Saharan Africa. The company addresses a clear market failure: transformational prosthetic technology exists, but is priced and distributed exclusively for Western markets, leaving a generation of African amputees with sporting ambition and no path to realise it.

MagnaStep's modular magnetic prosthetic targets a price of $2,000–$4,000 — up to 94% cheaper than Western equivalents — by leveraging regional supply chains and a modular design architecture. Distribution is embedded within existing disability services and rehabilitation centres, eliminating the cost and complexity of building new clinical infrastructure from scratch.

The company is seeking €2 million in seed funding to complete device prototyping, run a 50-athlete pilot programme in Rwanda, and sign anchor partnerships with disability service organisations in Rwanda and Uganda.

**Target:** Break-even by Year 3. €18M revenue by Year 5.

---

## 1. The Problem

Young amputees in sub-Saharan Africa — particularly those aged 15–30 who lost limbs due to agricultural accidents, landmines, or illness — have virtually no pathway into adaptive sport:

- High-performance magnetic prosthetics cost $15,000–$70,000 in Western markets
- Specialist fitting clinics exist only in Europe and North America
- No structured sports pathway exists from rehabilitation to competition in the region
- Less than 2% of people requiring prosthetic devices in low-income countries actually receive them (WHO, 2024)

**The market failure is not demand. It is supply, price, and geography.**

---

## 2. The Solution

### 2.1 The Device — MagnaStep Sport Prosthetic
- Modular magnetic osseointegration coupling
- Swappable sport-specific attachments: running blade, board binding, cycling clip
- Designed for African climate, terrain, and body profiles
- Manufactured via regional supply chains
- Target price: $2,000–$4,000

### 2.2 The Fitting Network
- Embedded within existing disability services and rehabilitation centres (Rwanda, Uganda, Kenya)
- MagnaStep trains local technicians — no need to travel to Europe
- Remote monitoring via basic smartphone app for ongoing adjustment
- Annual in-person check-in

### 2.3 The Sports Pathway
- Partnerships with national football federations, athletics boards, and schools
- Adaptive athletes train alongside able-bodied peers — same clubs, same coaches
- Clear progression: local club → national adaptive team → Paralympic qualification
- MagnaStep-certified coaches embedded in partner sports clubs

## 3. Market Analysis

### 3.1 Total Addressable Market (TAM)
**$1.8 billion** — Total African prosthetics market by 2030, growing at 7.2% CAGR (GlobalData, 2024). Driven by rising amputee population, expanding middle class, and increasing government disability services investment.

### 3.2 Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)
**$220 million** — Young amputees (15–35) with sporting aspirations in Rwanda, Uganda, and Kenya. Estimated 1.1 million individuals; approximately 20% with active interest in sport.

### 3.3 Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM — 5-Year Target)
**$28 million** — 7,000 devices at $2,000–$4,000 average selling price, targeting the highest-aspiration segment first across three countries.

*Note: Year 1 includes €2M seed funding used for R&D and operations, not shown as revenue.*

### 4.2 Pricing Model

MagnaStep operates a sliding-scale pricing model:

- **Subsidised Access:** Income-assessed; fully funded through NGO grants and impact investor capital. No device withheld for inability to pay.
- **Standard Programme:** $2,000–$3,500 depending on configuration. Includes 2 sport attachments and 12-month remote monitoring.
- **Institutional Pricing:** Negotiated bulk rates for disability services and sports organisations. Includes technician training.

### 4.3 Unit Economics (Year 3 Steady State)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Average selling price | $2,800 |
| Cost of goods sold | $1,100 |
| Gross margin per device | $1,700 (61%) |
| Technician training (amortised) | $150/device |
| Contribution margin | $1,550/device |
| Break-even volume | ~1,200 devices/year |

### 4.4 B-Corp Certification

MagnaStep is structured as a B-Corp. This legally protects the social mission as the company scales and receives institutional investment — the income-adjusted pricing model cannot be removed by investor pressure without breaching B-Corp obligations.

## 6. Marketing Campaign

### 6.1 Campaign Overview

**Campaign Name:** *"First Step"*

**Central idea:** Every great athlete has a first step. For Amara and thousands like her, MagnaStep is that step. The campaign celebrates the moment of possibility — not the disability, not the technology, but the human decision to try.

**Tone:** Bold. Aspirational. Inclusive. The language of sport, not charity.

**Audience:** Three distinct audiences, one shared message:

1. **Primary — Young amputees (15–30)** in Rwanda, Uganda, Kenya: *"This exists for you."*
2. **Secondary — Sports organisations and disability services:** *"Your athletes are waiting."*
3. **Tertiary — Impact investors and media:** *"The most defensible prosthetics business on the continent."*

---

### 6.2 Campaign Phases

#### Phase 1 — Seed the Story (Months 1–3)
**Objective:** Build awareness and emotional connection through Amara's story.

**Channels:**
- Launch MagnaStep landing page (index.html) with Amara's narrative
- Publish Gemini Veo 2-produced inspirational video (YouTube + social)
- Partner with Rwanda Media Group for earned press coverage
- Outreach to disability rights organisations for amplification

**Key content:**
- "Amara's First Run" — 90-second video (Gemini Veo 2)
- Long-form blog: *"Why we built this from Rwanda, not Silicon Valley"*
- Instagram/WhatsApp content series: short athlete stories (user-generated)

**KPI:** 50,000 video views, 5,000 landing page visits, 500 waitlist sign-ups

---

#### Phase 2 — Build the Community (Months 4–6)
**Objective:** Convert interest into a community of future athletes and partners.

**Channels:**
- WhatsApp broadcast group (primary channel in target markets — low data)
- Instagram Reels (short-form athlete content)
- Partnerships with 3 national sports federations for co-announcement
- Press tour: TechCabal, CNN Africa, BBC Africa

**Key content:**
- "50 Athletes Wanted" — open call for pilot programme participants
- Partner announcement series: disability services and sports federations
- Founder story content: *"Why we left our jobs to build this"*

**KPI:** 1,000 pilot applicants, 3 partnership MOUs signed, 15 press mentions

---

#### Phase 3 — Prove It (Months 7–18)
**Objective:** Use pilot athletes as living proof of concept — marketing through outcomes.

**Channels:**
- Documentary mini-series (3 episodes, YouTube + social): following 5 pilot athletes
- Athletes as brand ambassadors — organic content, no scripts
- Sports organisation events: presence at national athletics competitions
- Impact report published: clinical outcomes, athlete testimonials

**Key content:**
- "The First 50" documentary series
- Athlete-generated social content (training, competitions, daily life)
- Annual impact report: *"Year One — What We Learned"*

**KPI:** 500K total video views, 10K community members, 3 athletes competing nationally

## 7. Risk Analysis

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Device regulatory approval delayed | Medium | High | Begin regulatory pathway in parallel with prototyping; engage Rwanda FDA early |
| Clinical complications in pilot | Low | Very High | Clinical lead oversight; strict inclusion criteria; informed consent protocol |
| Partner disability services withdraw | Low | High | MOUs with 3+ partners; no single-partner dependency |
| Price target not achievable | Medium | High | Supply chain validated before scaling; modular design allows cost-adjustment |
| Competitor enters market | Low | Medium | B-Corp structure + community trust as moat; first-mover advantage |
| Exchange rate / economic instability | Medium | Medium | Revenue in USD; costs partly in local currency — natural hedge |
